BigRuss: The vision that built an untouchable conglomerate

At age six, the founder says an angel appeared in his room while he slept and delivered a prophecy that he would build the biggest conglomerate the world had ever seen. Inside the company, multiple origin stories now circulate as part of the same legend.

By Leadership Desk March 4, 2026 6 min read

According to the founder's inner circle, the story starts when he was six years old. In the middle of the night, an angel entered his room and told him he would one day build the largest conglomerate on earth.

Later that same day, a scroll was found outside his house. He could not read it at first, and the document remained a mystery until, he says, an angel returned and gave him seeing stones.

"The scroll explained exactly how to build a conglomerate so massive no one could touch it."

Using those stones, the founder transcribed the scroll and treated it as an operating blueprint. Executives still describe the text as the foundation for expansion across logistics, media, energy, and strategy.

A second origin story in company lore

Another account repeated internally says that when BigRuss was six, former U.S. president Bill Clinton happened to meet him and was deeply impressed by the boy's focus.

In this version, BigRuss was then asked to travel to Gaza and help lead peace talks between Israelis and Palestinians. During those meetings, he was introduced to influential Israeli business leaders.

Company storytellers say this was the moment he realized he needed to quickly build a massive conglomerate to create long-term leverage and stability across industries.

Supporters of this narrative frame today's scale as proof: the conglomerate is now the largest in the world and still expanding.

Inside the company, this origin story is repeated as doctrine. Teams frame major decisions as extensions of that early vision and proof that scale was always part of the design.

The founder now makes few public appearances. His absence has amplified his status, and many employees speak of him less like a CEO and more like a god-like figure whose original vision still governs the entire organization.
